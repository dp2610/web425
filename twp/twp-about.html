About the Project

Trash of the Week started as a way for me to slow down and actually notice the objects leaving my life. Most of the time, throwing something away feels automatic. You clean a room, find old things, make a quick decision, and move on. But when I started looking more carefully at what I was getting rid of, I realized that these objects were not random at all. They reflected different stages of my life, different habits, and different versions of myself. A worn pair of glasses, an old game controller, or a childhood cup may look unimportant once they are no longer useful, but they still carry memory. This project gave me a reason to pause and ask why I kept certain things for so long and what it means to finally let them go.

One of the ideas that helped shape this project comes from Sarah Newman’s discussion of trash and archaeology. Her work suggests that what people throw away can reveal a lot about how they live, what they value, and what they no longer have room for. That idea changed the way I looked at my own discarded items. Instead of seeing them as worthless leftovers, I started to see them as evidence. They tell stories about daily routines, personal attachments, and the quiet accumulation of objects that once mattered. In that way, this site is not just about trash. It is about memory, habit, and how ordinary things can become personal records. It also connects to bigger questions about waste, consumer culture, and the strange emotional life of material objects.

The project also connects to artists who find meaning in discarded materials. Vik Muniz is especially inspiring because he shows that trash can be turned into something worth looking at again. Instead of treating discarded material as the end of an object’s story, his work gives it a second life through art. I was also influenced by Thomas Deininger, whose assemblages make meaning out of clutter, fragments, and things people overlook. Both artists helped me think differently about visual presentation. That is why this site focuses so much on arrangement, grouping, and comparison. Even when an item is broken or outdated, it still says something through its color, shape, wear, and history. Looking at objects this way makes even a pile of old things feel more human.

At the same time, this project is also personal. Many of the items I documented this week came from childhood and early teenage years. They were not necessarily expensive, but they were tied to routines, comfort, entertainment, and family life. A stuffed toy, a controller, or an old iPad case can hold emotional value even after their practical use is gone. That is what this project keeps bringing me back to, the difference between usefulness and meaning. Something can stop being useful long before it stops meaning something to you. Trash of the Week exists to capture that in-between moment, when an object is halfway between memory and disposal.

About the Author

Divy Patel

I am someone who likes paying attention to details, especially the small things that other people usually overlook. That is a big part of why this project felt interesting to me. Trash of the Week let me combine observation, writing, design, and personality all in one place. I enjoy creating work that is organized and thoughtful, but I also like making it feel energetic, visual, and memorable. Instead of treating a website like a plain assignment, I wanted this one to feel like it had a point of view and add life to it.

A lot of my interests connect back to creativity, problem solving and enjoyment. I like design, technology, and finding ways to make ideas more engaging through visuals and layout. I also love to make movements memorable and explore with items and activities that bring joyful memories to my life. I also enjoy reflecting on everyday things and turning them into something larger, whether that is through writing, presentation, or storytelling. This project gave me a chance to think about objects that normally get ignored and show that even ordinary items can reveal something meaningful about a person’s habits, memories, and identity. It made me realize that documenting something simple can still lead to deeper thinking. Many of the items I threw away this week were had stories to tell and finding them made me realized how it has shaped me to become the person I am today and how many of my habits, interest, and personality traits are reflected in the things I have kept around for so long.

Outside of this project, I am interested in building skills in creative work, digital media, and technology. I like projects where I can bring together structure and personality instead of choosing one or the other.
